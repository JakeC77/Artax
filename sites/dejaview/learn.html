The case for a knowledge graph

Why your knowledge is broken
— and how to fix it

You know you saved that thing somewhere. You remember reading it, it was important. And now it’s just… gone — buried under open tabs and three apps you half-use.

Your knowledge lives everywhere — and because of that, it’s effectively nowhere.
Sound familiar?

You’re not forgetful.
Your tools just weren’t built for this.

These aren’t brain failures — they’re tool failures. Every one of these has probably happened to you this week.

😵

You remember having a conversation with someone about a project, but you can’t find it — not in Slack, not in email, not in your notes.

🤔

You know you made a decision six months ago — but you can’t for the life of you remember why, and now you’re second-guessing it again.

📅

You have a meeting with someone and you draw a complete blank on what you discussed last time. You wing it and hope they don’t notice.

📋

The real problem

Your tools think in lists.
Your brain thinks in connections.

Spreadsheets, note apps, databases — they all store things in rows and columns. Neat little boxes, organized by category, sorted by date, filed by folder.

But that’s not how knowledge actually works. Knowledge isn’t about items — it’s about relationships. It’s about how things connect, influence each other, and weave together into context.

A spreadsheet can tell you Jake works at Acme Corp. Full stop. It can’t tell you that Jake introduced you to Sarah, who’s working on Project Phoenix, which is using the same vendor as your other project — and that’s probably why the timeline is slipping.

“A spreadsheet stores facts. A knowledge graph stores understanding.”

When you can follow the connections, you stop searching and start discovering.
Same data. Completely different power. Left: a list. Right: a knowledge graph.
What is a knowledge graph?

Your brain, but it never forgets.

Nodes = things (people, projects, ideas)
Edges = relationships (knows, decided, uses)
Paths = insight you didn’t know you had

Think of it like this: a node is anything that exists — a person, a project, a decision, an idea, a meeting. A relationship is how two things connect: knows, works_at, decided, depends_on, introduced_by.

The magic isn’t in the individual facts. It’s in what you can discover by following the connections. You find things you didn’t know you knew. Context you forgot you had.

That’s a knowledge graph. Not a fancier file system — an actual second brain.

A real knowledge graph: people, projects, decisions — all connected.
